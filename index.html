These counters are analytical estimates, not real-time counts of confirmed fatalities, confirmed injuries, or direct Russian government spending. VSL estimates the economic value of mortality losses; VSI estimates the economic value of nonfatal battlefield injuries.

Independent research project based on transparent methodology and open-source data.

Methodology

This counter estimates the economic value of Russian military fatalities using the Value of a Statistical Life (VSL) framework. VSL is commonly used in public policy and cost-benefit analysis to estimate the monetary value of reducing mortality risk. It does not represent the moral value or market price of an individual life.

1. VSL transfer approach

Because Russia does not have a transparent and reliable domestic wartime VSL estimate, this project uses a benefit-transfer method from the VSL literature, especially the approach associated with Masterman and Viscusi . The U.S. benchmark VSL is transferred to Russia by adjusting for income differences between the United States and Russia using GNI per capita.

VSLRussia,2025 = VSLUS,2025 × (GNIRussia,2025 / GNIUS,2025)ε

2. Inputs used in this estimate

  • U.S. DOT VSL benchmark, 2025: $14.2 million .
  • Russia GNI per capita, 2025: $15,960 .
  • U.S. GNI per capita, 2025: $88,810 .
  • Russia/U.S. income ratio: 0.180.
  • Income elasticity: ε = 1.0.
  • Transferred Russian VSL, 2025: approximately $2.55 million per fatality.
  • Fatality baseline: 500,000 Russian military fatalities as of June 1, 2026 , based on recent British intelligence statements, including GCHQ and FCDO reporting.
  • Baseline aggregate VSL estimate: approximately $1.28 trillion as of June 1, 2026.

3. Aggregate calculation

Aggregate VSL Loss = 500,000 × $2.55 million ≈ $1.28 trillion

The 2025 transferred Russian VSL is applied to the cumulative fatality baseline because 2025 is the latest available full-year VSL and income input. A more precise year-by-year valuation would require reliable annual fatality totals, which are not available with the same confidence as the cumulative estimate.

4. Live counter logic

The baseline VSL value is $1.28 trillion as of June 1, 2026. After that date, the live counter increases at the average historical rate implied by the period from February 24, 2022 to June 1, 2026. This creates a continuously updated estimate synchronized to the current date and time.

Current VSL Estimate = $1.28 trillion + seconds since June 1, 2026 × $9,508.87

This is an extrapolated analytical measure. It should not be interpreted as a confirmed real-time casualty count, a direct fiscal cost, or actual Russian government expenditure.

This counter estimates the economic value of Russian nonfatal battlefield casualties using a Value of Statistical Injury (VSI) framework. While VSL values mortality risk, VSI values nonfatal injury risk. This distinction matters because nonfatal injuries can impose long-term costs through medical treatment, disability, lost productivity, reduced quality of life, and family care burdens.

1. Nonfatal casualty baseline

The model uses 900,000 Russian nonfatal battlefield casualties as the baseline injury estimate. This number is not presented as a verified medical registry of wounded personnel. It is a modeling assumption derived from broader casualty estimates and kept internally consistent with the 500,000 fatality baseline.

Nonfatal Casualties = 1.4 million total casualties − 500,000 fatalities = 900,000

The total casualty benchmark is anchored in the CSIS estimate of roughly 1.4 million Russian battlefield casualties .

2. Injury-severity categories

Russia does not publish reliable public data on battlefield injuries by medical severity, diagnosis, disability status, or return-to-duty outcome. For that reason, the model uses a scenario-based severity distribution:

  • Light injuries: 50% = 450,000 casualties.
  • Moderate injuries: 35% = 315,000 casualties.
  • Severe injuries: 15% = 135,000 casualties.

To avoid false precision, the model collapses the DOT injury-severity scale into three broad wartime injury tiers: light, moderate, and severe. These are economic modeling categories, not medical diagnoses.

3. DOT injury fractions and VSI values

The valuation uses selected U.S. Department of Transportation injury-severity fractions of VSL. The selected DOT fractions are:

  • Light injury: 0.003 × Russian VSL.
  • Moderate injury: 0.105 × Russian VSL.
  • Severe injury: 0.593 × Russian VSL.
VSIi = Russian VSL2025 × DOT Injury Fractioni

Using the 2025 transferred Russian VSL of approximately $2.55 million, the resulting VSI values are:

  • Light injury: $7,650 per injury.
  • Moderate injury: $267,750 per injury.
  • Severe injury: $1,512,150 per injury.

4. Aggregate VSI calculation

Aggregate VSI = Σ Number of Injuriesi × VSIi
  • Light: 450,000 × $7,650 = $3.44 billion.
  • Moderate: 315,000 × $267,750 = $84.34 billion.
  • Severe: 135,000 × $1,512,150 = $204.14 billion.
  • Total baseline VSI: approximately $291.92 billion.

Most of the aggregate VSI estimate comes from the severe-injury category. Although severe injuries account for only 15 percent of estimated nonfatal casualties, they account for the majority of the aggregate VSI estimate because the economic harm associated with severe or disability-level injury is much larger than the harm associated with temporary injury.

5. Live counter logic

The baseline VSI value is $291.92 billion as of June 1, 2026. After that date, the live counter increases at the average historical rate implied by the period from February 24, 2022 to June 1, 2026.

Current VSI Estimate = $291.92 billion + seconds since June 1, 2026 × $2,168.62

This is an extrapolated analytical measure. It should not be interpreted as a confirmed real-time injury count, a Russian medical registry, a direct fiscal cost, or actual Russian government expenditure.

6. IMF expenditure comparison

The comparison section uses IMF World Economic Outlook benchmarks to show the live VSL + VSI estimate relative to annual general government expenditure. The benchmark is calculated as nominal GDP in U.S. dollars multiplied by general government total expenditure as a percentage of GDP.

IMF Expenditure Benchmark = GDP, current prices, U.S. dollars × General Government Total Expenditure, % of GDP

The comparison ratio is updated every second using the live VSL + VSI estimate.

Ratio = Current VSL + VSI Estimate / IMF Expenditure Benchmark
